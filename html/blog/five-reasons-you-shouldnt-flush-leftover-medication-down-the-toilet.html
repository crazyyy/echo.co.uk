Why you shouldn't flush your medication down the toilet

February 01st, 2017 by Stephanie Hall

I recently had a conversation with some friends about the current state of the environment. That might seem a bit nerd-like to some of you reading this, but most young people I know are environmentally aware these days, so this kind of conversation tends to crop up a lot.

After working our way through the obligatory tree-hugging stories of spiritual enlightenment and Pocahontas-level oneness with nature that we experienced on our Duke of Edinburgh expeditions, we hit on a topic that I found really interesting, considering my job as a content writer for Echo.

One of my friends mentioned that she always feels really guilty for wasting stuff. And by ‘stuff’, she was referring to broken troll dolls (90s nostalgia right there), used toilet paper, the hair from her very own head and the mouldy cheese sitting in her fridge. You name it; she feels guilty about throwing it away. But then she said she feels guilty about wasting her medicine.

And that got me thinking: how much medicine do we actually waste in the UK? Is it on the same scale as toilet paper? Surely not...

I followed up with some research once I got home, because yes, I’m a geek, and I found some really interesting information that I’ve decided to share in this article.

So if you’ve got a burgeoning pile of medicines wasting away at home and you’re thinking of flushing them down the loo just to get rid of them, please read on, because we really need to get to grips with our medicine waste if we want to save our money, our environment, and most importantly, our health.

Adding to NHS debt

According to a study published by the Department of Health, an estimated £300 million worth of medical products are wasted in England every year. With the standard cost of UK medical prescriptions totalling £8.40 per person, £300 million amounts to around 37 million individual prescriptions.


Where it gets even more interesting, and saddening, is where you take account of the fact that £461 million was the figure provided by NHS Improvement when they reported on the total NHS deficit in the first quarter of 2016-2017.

You don’t have to be a Pythagorean-level mathematician to understand that £300 million is more than 65% of £461 million.

By ordering our medicines earlier than we need to and ordering more medicine than we need to at one time (perhaps unintentionally), we are adding to the huge debt plaguing our public healthcare systems. Which means we’re pretty much shooting ourselves in the foot.

Aside from bringing medicinal waste to your attention via startling facts and wittily written prose (*wink*), Echo is helping put an end to this kind of wastage by sending reminders through our app prompting you to order your medication only when it’s due to be refilled.

We stagger the reminders for each of your medications, so that you only end up ordering the ones you need when you need them. This helps prevent you from stockpiling your medications, which in turn ensures there are no medications lying around your home for anyone to flush down the toilet, along with our chances of saving the NHS from financial ruin.

Damaging the environment with toxic medication

It will come as no surprise to you to hear that flushing your expired medication down the toilet means it will ultimately end up in our water supplies, and in the homes of our favourite fishy friends Dory, Nemo and Flounder. (Re-watch Disney’s 'The Little Mermaid' if the last reference escaped you, you animation heathen).

Gwynne Lyons, Director of Policy at CHEM Trust, has drawn attention to the fact that humans tend to excrete between 30-90% of the medicines we take, and she has said that:

"With so many medicines now being found in our rivers, action on all fronts is needed to protect wildlife and drinking water."

CHEM Trust has also sampled fish contaminated with birth control pills, antidepressants, sedatives, antibiotics, painkillers, and anti-cancer drugs, and this means that UK amphibians, fish and crustaceans alike all stand to experience disruptive alterations in their daily habits, reproductive patterns and standard developments, which could put their lives in jeopardy.


Flushing your medicines down the loo basically means you’re flushing toxins directly into UK aquatic wildlife, so if you enjoy strolling alongside the River Thames and seeing a school of cod (or coli, as is more likely to be the case), you need to start disposing of your medications in a more environmentally sustainable way.

Don’t fret: I’ve got some advice on how to do that coming up after this last point about the dangers of flushing your medicine down the toilet.

Putting our health at risk

According to studies listed on the Cancer Research UK website, exposure to increased levels of oestrogen while in the womb can be linked to increased risks of testicular cancer.

‘What does that have to do with flushing medicine down the loo?’, I hear you ask.

Well, scientists have been researching the amount of oestrogen in our water supplies, and they are growing increasingly concerned that oestrogen-contaminated water can be linked to increased cases of testicular cancer in the UK.

So while you may think that flushing your surplus menopause medication is harmless, it could turn out that it’s actually increasing your husband, brother, son or father’s chances of developing a terminal illness, which is a horrifying thought.

The sizeable supplies of other chemicals like chlorine, fluoride and even cocaine already circling around our drains and threatening to harm our collective health means that adding prescription medicines to the bubbling cocktail of sewage is probably one of the worst things we could do.


How do I safely dispose of old or unused medication?

The best way of ensuring any expired or surplus medication is disposed of safely and responsibly is to return it to your local pharmacy.

UK pharmacies are obliged to accept return medication and they will arrange for it's safe disposal. (You can read more about this on the BMA and NHS web pages.) 

Disposing of your medication through your pharmacy has the added bonus of ensuring that you don't have any expired medication sitting at home which could be taken by someone you live with; potentially leading to a critical situation.

If you take one thing away from this article, let it be this: flushed medication does not vanish into a magical pool of nothingness. It may well return to your home and enter your body through the water you drink.

This makes taking your unwanted medication down to your local pharmacy once you’re done with it essential if you want to give yourself, and everyone else around you, the highest chance of safeguarding against any unexpected monetary, environmental, or health complications.
